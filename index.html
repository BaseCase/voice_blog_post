Speaking Up: A How-To Guide

by Casey Brant

Note

This is the written version of a talk I gave at Adorable. The slides I used are shown here next to what are essentially expanded speaker notes. The interactive portion of the talk doesn’t translate that well to text, but I did my best to explain the exercises. If you have questions, feel free to reach out to me at casey@adorable.io or send me a tweet!

Thanks for joining me! Today I want to talk about something we don’t think about that much but that affects us constantly: our voices.

Let’s get started.

The first part of this talk is pretty standard; I’ll give you a little bit of background information to prepare for the second portion. After that, things will get a little weird as we actually do some vocal exercises together as a group.

But first, let’s talk about the goals for this talk. I want to be clear that, though the title may suggest otherwise, this is not about public speaking or communication skills. It’s much simpler than that. I’m also not here to change anything about you or your voice. Quite the opposite, actually: I want to help you share your voice as it is more effectively.

My starting point when putting together this talk was very simple: sometimes we are not loud enough! We’ve probably all had the experience of being in a meeting—especially one involving teleconferencing software—and we get interrupted by someone saying they can’t hear us. So I want to help you become more aware of your voice and offer your some ideas that might help you respond to that feedback in an effective way.

A quick disclaimer.

This doesn’t apply so much as a blog post, but for the live talk I made sure to note that—since everyone has a different level of comfort with their own voice—it is totally OK to just chill out during the interactive exercises portion of the talk.

In this first section, I’d like to go over some background information that I think we probably all know at some level but may not think about consciously while we’re speaking.

Voices are a kind of sound, so let’s start with sound. You can think of sound as being composed of three main parts:

  1. Vibrations, generated by some source and traveling through a medium (usually the air)
  2. Ears, receiving those vibrations in the air and converting them into input that we can use
  3. Brains, turning that input into meaningful data.

If you’re missing any of those three components, you don’t have a meaningful sound. For the physicists in the audience who now silently hate me, please not that I’m intentionally framing/abstracting things in a way that makes them useful for thinking about the voice; I realize that this is not a 100% accurate view of the universe.

A few other tidbits about sound that we want to keep in mind while speaking:

  • We are by and large a visually oriented species, and so we don’t always think about the fact that sound loses fidelity much more quickly than sight. This matters especially in the case when we’re teleconferencing: get right on top of a mic and there’s no problem, but move just a few feet away and suddenly you are inaudible.
  • Those sound vibrations are changed by the media they are traveling through. You’ve probably had the experience of hearing a recording of yourself and thinking “Who the heck is that?” Your voice sounds quite a bit different to you than it does to people outside of your own skull.
  • We can think of the sounds we produce as directional. If you want someone to hear you, you should point yourself at them. If you look down, or off to the side, or into a computer monitor, those sound vibrations are not going to get to their ears as effectively.

Now let’s talk about the voice specifically.
